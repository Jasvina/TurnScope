# Signal Cartography

Signal Cartography treats invisible systems as landscapes that can be surveyed, mapped, and made navigable. Its visual language is built from lines of causality, dense but controlled event strata, and precisely labeled zones that imply an expert instrument panel rather than a decorative poster. The work should feel meticulously crafted, the product of deep expertise, with every axis, node, and gap placed through painstaking attention.

Space is divided like a field notebook from an imaginary observatory: large quiet margins hold the composition in tension while the center contains a compressed map of motion. Scale shifts should feel intentional: small marks accumulate into large meaning, and a few dominant forms anchor the viewer. The final artifact must look labored over with care, like someone at the top of their field spent countless hours calibrating it.

Color is functional, not ornamental. Warm paper and hard ink carry trust; blue, amber, green, and red act as signal classes. The palette must be restrained enough to feel rigorous and vivid enough to be remembered. Craftsmanship is expressed through exact alignment, measured rhythm, and the sense that every repeated mark has a reason.

Typography is sparse and instrument-like. Words appear as labels, callsigns, and short declarations; the image, not prose, carries the argument. The poster should never explain too much. It should reveal a system through composition, with master-level execution and the confidence of a technical artifact that has become art.
